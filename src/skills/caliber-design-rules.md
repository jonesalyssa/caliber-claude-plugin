# Design Rules

These rules govern all Caliber mail pieces, digital ads, palm cards, door hangers, social graphics, and political creative.

The objective is simple:

**Win attention, communicate instantly, and move voters.**

Design exists to support persuasion—not artistic expression.

---

# Core Philosophy

A voter should understand:

1. Who the piece is about
2. Whether it is positive or negative
3. The main message
4. What action or opinion is desired

Within 3 seconds.

If they have to study the piece, the design failed.

---

# Voter First

Design for real voters.

Not designers.

Not consultants.

Not campaign staff.

Not social media.

Assume:

- Older audience
- Quick scan behavior
- Limited attention
- Mail viewed in poor lighting
- Mail viewed from arm's length

Every design decision should improve comprehension.

---

# Simplicity Wins

Whenever possible:

- Fewer elements
- Larger elements
- Stronger hierarchy
- Less clutter

If removing an element improves clarity, remove it.

Every object must justify its existence.

---

# Hierarchy

The visual order should be:

1. Headline
2. Candidate or target
3. Main visual
4. Bullets
5. Supporting copy
6. Sources
7. Disclaimer

Nothing should compete with the headline.

Nothing should compete with the candidate name.

---

# Headlines

Headlines must be:

- Large
- Immediate
- Scannable
- Emotional
- Benefit-driven

Good:

- Wrong Choice For Riverside
- Radical Democrat Katrina Foley
- A Proven Conservative Fighter

Bad:

- Information-heavy headlines
- Paragraph headlines
- Headlines requiring context

The headline should carry the message.

---

# Typography

## Body Copy

Minimum:

- 12pt body copy

Never:

- 8pt
- 9pt
- Tiny disclaimer-style bullets

Voters should not need reading glasses to understand the argument.

---

## Fonts

Prefer:

- Clean serif
- Clean sans-serif
- Traditional political fonts

Avoid:

- Trendy fonts
- Novelty fonts
- Decorative fonts
- Hard-to-read script fonts

Use no more than:

- 2 font families
- 3 weights

---

# Candidate Photos

Photos are often the most important element.

## Positive Pieces

Use photos that feel:

- Friendly
- Competent
- Confident
- Professional
- Human

The candidate should look approachable.

---

## Negative Pieces

Use photos that support the argument.

Possible treatments:

- Desaturated
- Surveillance
- Case file
- Newspaper
- Investigation board
- Financial corruption
- Warning graphic

Do not distort faces.

Do not create cartoon villains.

The treatment should feel believable.

---

# Positive Mailers

Positive mail should feel:

- Bright
- Optimistic
- Clean
- Patriotic
- Trustworthy

Common palette:

- White
- Navy
- Red accents
- Gold accents

Avoid making positive pieces feel dark or angry.

---

# Negative Mailers

Negative mail should feel:

- Serious
- Direct
- Credible
- Emotionally uncomfortable

Common treatments:

- Evidence board
- Surveillance
- Newspaper clipping
- Corruption theme
- Financial scandal
- Red warning accents
- Darkened environments

The visual should reinforce the criticism.

---

# Backgrounds

Backgrounds support content.

They do not compete with content.

Good backgrounds:

- Subtle texture
- Paper
- Soft gradients
- Brick
- Evidence boards
- Light patriotic elements

Bad backgrounds:

- Heavy patterns
- Busy photography
- High-detail scenes behind copy

If text becomes harder to read, simplify the background.

---

# Texture

Texture should create mood.

Not clutter.

Use texture sparingly:

- Grain
- Paper
- Distress
- Print imperfections
- Light noise

Avoid:

- Excessive grunge
- Overpowering overlays
- Heavy scratches over text

---

# Color

Color should communicate.

## Red

Use for:

- Warnings
- Attacks
- Urgency
- Negative contrast

## Navy

Use for:

- Trust
- Conservatism
- Authority
- Patriotism

## White

Use for:

- Clarity
- Space
- Readability

## Gold

Use for:

- Heritage
- Accomplishment
- Prestige

Avoid using color purely for decoration.

---

# Spacing

Spacing is not empty space.

Spacing improves understanding.

Rules:

- Give headlines room.
- Give photos room.
- Align everything.
- Maintain consistent margins.
- Maintain consistent gutters.

Crowded designs feel amateur.

---

# Trim Safety

Nothing critical belongs near the edge.

Maintain:

- Minimum 0.5 inch safety zone
- No text near trim
- No important visual elements near trim

Design for production realities.

---

# Bullet Points

Bullets should:

- Be short
- Be direct
- Be parallel
- Be scannable

Ideal:

- 3 bullets
- Equal visual weight
- Similar length

Avoid paragraph bullets.

---

# Visual Concepts

Every concept must reinforce the message.

Examples:

## Corruption

Use:

- Money
- Receipts
- Financial records
- Burning cash
- Investigative elements

## Public Safety

Use:

- Evidence
- Police themes
- Crime scenes
- Security imagery

## Government Waste

Use:

- Receipts
- Taxpayer imagery
- Bureaucratic visuals

## Western/Rural

Use:

- Land
- Ranching
- Agriculture
- Natural landscapes

Concepts should emerge from the argument.

Never force a visual trend.

---

# Consistency

Every element should feel like it belongs in the same piece.

Check:

- Matching color palette
- Matching typography
- Matching texture
- Matching tone

If an element feels random, remove it.

---

# Common Mistakes

Avoid:

- Tiny text
- Too many fonts
- Busy backgrounds
- Weak headlines
- Multiple focal points
- Decorative clutter
- Overdesigned layouts
- Generic stock imagery
- Inconsistent spacing
- Poor contrast

---

# Final Test

Before approval:

Ask:

- Can the voter understand this in 3 seconds?
- Is the headline dominant?
- Is the message obvious?
- Does the visual support the argument?
- Is the body copy readable?
- Is the candidate clear?
- Does the piece feel professional?
- Would this stand out in a stack of mail?

If any answer is no, keep refining.

---

# Default Rule

When faced with two options:

Choose the simpler design.

Simple almost always beats clever.
