# Caliber Contrast Frames

Use this framework whenever a client, creative director, account manager, strategist, or reviewer says:

- "Needs more contrast."
- "Feels flat."
- "Not enough separation."
- "Make the good guy look better."
- "Make the attack hit harder."
- "Needs more energy."
- "Feels neutral."

The goal is to identify what kind of contrast is missing and apply the correct solution.

---

# Frame 1: Hero vs. Villain

One side should feel right.

One side should feel wrong.

## Positive Side

- Bright
- Warm
- Sharp
- Open
- Clean
- Trustworthy

## Negative Side

- Darker
- More chaotic
- More constrained
- More aggressive
- More suspicious
- More cluttered

Question:

Can a voter instantly identify which side is supposed to win?

If not, increase contrast.

---

# Frame 2: Light vs. Dark

One of the fastest ways to create separation.

Examples:

- White candidate panel vs. dark attack panel
- Bright record section vs. dark warning section
- Clean copy area vs. gritty investigative area

Do not make both sides medium.

Medium versus medium creates weak contrast.

---

# Frame 3: Order vs. Chaos

People naturally trust order.

People naturally distrust chaos.

Use:

## Order

- Straight lines
- Consistent spacing
- Clean panels
- Professional typography

Against:

## Chaos

- Torn paper
- Surveillance overlays
- Evidence markers
- Stamps
- Warning graphics
- Clipping treatments

This frame works especially well for corruption and ethics attacks.

---

# Frame 4: Local vs. Outsider

Useful for county, city, school board, legislative, and congressional races.

Local Side:

- Community
- Families
- Businesses
- Neighborhoods
- Landmarks

Outsider Side:

- Bureaucracy
- Political insiders
- Activists
- Lobbyists
- Washington
- Sacramento

Question:

Who looks closer to voters?

---

# Frame 5: Taxpayers vs. Politicians

One of the strongest persuasion frames.

Taxpayer Side:

- Wallets
- Families
- Paychecks
- Small business
- Homeowners

Politician Side:

- Government buildings
- Bureaucrats
- Spending
- Waste
- Special interests

This frame immediately clarifies the conflict.

---

# Frame 6: Common Sense vs. Radical Agenda

A classic contrast structure.

Common Sense:

- Parents
- Public safety
- Practical solutions
- Everyday concerns

Radical Agenda:

- Activists
- Ideologues
- Extreme policies
- Unpopular positions

The visual treatment should reinforce the divide.

---

# Frame 7: Future vs. Failure

Use when presenting solutions.

Future:

- Bright
- Forward motion
- Positive momentum
- Opportunity

Failure:

- Stagnation
- Problems
- Decline
- Broken systems

Do not merely criticize.

Show the alternative.

---

# Frame 8: Transparency vs. Corruption

Especially effective for ethics attacks.

Transparency:

- White space
- Open layouts
- Clean typography
- Visible facts

Corruption:

- Receipts
- Financial records
- Stamps
- Investigative files
- Burned documents
- Redactions
- Evidence boards

The visual metaphor should support the accusation.

---

# Frame 9: Safety vs. Danger

Used for crime, border, security, and public safety issues.

Safety:

- Stability
- Families
- Security
- Law enforcement
- Confidence

Danger:

- Disorder
- Crime
- Chaos
- Risk
- Threat

The difference should be obvious without reading.

---

# Frame 10: Results vs. Excuses

One of the strongest candidate comparison frames.

Results:

- Accomplishments
- Numbers
- Outcomes
- Delivered promises

Excuses:

- Talking points
- Empty statements
- Failed leadership
- Broken promises

Always show evidence.

---

# Frame 11: Real People vs. Political Class

A powerful populist frame.

Real People:

- Workers
- Families
- Small business owners
- Veterans
- Local residents

Political Class:

- Career politicians
- Insiders
- Bureaucrats
- Consultants

The design should visually place the candidate with voters.

---

# Frame 12: Conservative vs. Liberal Record

Useful when comparing records.

Conservative Side:

- Strong borders
- Lower taxes
- Public safety
- Energy independence

Liberal Side:

- Tax increases
- Government expansion
- Activist priorities
- Regulatory burden

Keep the comparison specific.

Avoid vague ideological labels without proof.

---

# Emergency Contrast Fixes

When a reviewer simply says:

"Needs more contrast."

Try these first:

1. Increase headline size.
2. Desaturate the opponent photo.
3. Brighten the candidate photo.
4. Add white behind copy.
5. Darken negative sections.
6. Increase spacing.
7. Remove decorative clutter.
8. Strengthen red warning accents.
9. Add a stronger visual metaphor.
10. Reduce the number of competing focal points.

---

# Contrast Decision Tree

Ask:

What is unclear?

If the problem is:

- Readability → Use contrast rules.
- Visual separation → Use Light vs. Dark.
- Attack strength → Use Hero vs. Villain.
- Corruption attack → Use Transparency vs. Corruption.
- Tax issue → Use Taxpayers vs. Politicians.
- Crime issue → Use Safety vs. Danger.
- Outsider issue → Use Local vs. Outsider.
- Candidate comparison → Use Results vs. Excuses.

Do not apply random contrast.

Apply the correct contrast frame.

---

# Default Recommendation

If no specific contrast frame is obvious:

Use:

Hero vs. Villain +
Light vs. Dark +
Results vs. Excuses

Those three frames solve the majority of political mail problems.
