# Caliber Fact Check

Use these rules whenever generating political copy, mailers, digital ads, social posts, fundraising materials, earned media, press releases, talking points, opposition research summaries, or candidate comparison pieces.

The purpose of this system is not to determine whether a policy position is good or bad.

The purpose is to ensure every factual claim can survive scrutiny from:

* Reporters
* Opponents
* Lawyers
* Campaign staff
* Voters
* Platform reviewers
* Independent fact-checkers

A strong political message becomes useless if the underlying claim cannot be defended.

---

# Core Rule

Every factual claim must answer:

**"How do we know that?"**

If the answer is unclear, rewrite, source, qualify, or remove the claim.

---

# Claim Categories

Not all claims require the same level of proof.

---

## Category 1: Objective Facts

Examples:

* Vote records
* Bill sponsorships
* Campaign donations
* Public statements
* Attendance records
* Tax increases
* Government spending figures
* Criminal convictions
* Election results

These claims require direct sourcing.

Preferred sources:

* Government records
* Official voting records
* Court documents
* Financial disclosures
* Public transcripts
* Campaign finance filings

Highest confidence category.

---

## Category 2: Record-Based Claims

Examples:

* Supported higher taxes
* Opposed parental rights legislation
* Backed government spending increases
* Voted against law enforcement funding

These claims must connect directly to evidence.

Always verify:

* Vote
* Bill
* Resolution
* Public statement
* Sponsorship
* Co-sponsorship

Do not assume support.

Document support.

---

## Category 3: Interpretation Claims

Examples:

* Chose activists over families
* Put politics ahead of public safety
* Prioritized special interests

These are persuasive conclusions.

They require:

1. Supporting evidence
2. Reasonable connection
3. Defensible interpretation

Interpretation must be grounded in facts.

---

## Category 4: Opinion Claims

Examples:

* Wrong choice for Riverside
* Failed leadership
* Out of touch
* Radical agenda

These are opinions.

Opinions generally require less proof.

However:

The supporting facts behind the opinion must still be accurate.

---

# Evidence Standards

Use the strongest available evidence.

Preferred order:

1. Official government documents
2. Official vote records
3. Official candidate statements
4. Campaign websites
5. Public video/transcripts
6. Court filings
7. Major news organizations
8. Local news organizations
9. Credible advocacy organizations
10. Secondary commentary

The stronger the attack, the stronger the evidence required.

---

# Never Assume

Do not infer:

* Intent
* Motivation
* Knowledge
* Secret plans

Bad:

"Foley wanted to hurt taxpayers."

Better:

"Foley supported higher spending."

The action can be proven.

The motive often cannot.

---

# Avoid Mind Reading

Never present assumptions as facts.

Bad:

"He knew the policy would fail."

Bad:

"She intended to raise costs."

Better:

"He voted for the policy."

Better:

"She supported the proposal."

Stay with provable actions.

---

# Verify Quotes

Every quote must be:

* Exact
* Complete
* Properly attributed
* Contextually accurate

Never:

* Rewrite quotes
* Invent quotes
* Shorten quotes in misleading ways

When uncertain:

Paraphrase instead.

---

# Verify Statistics

Always confirm:

* Original source
* Date
* Geography
* Methodology
* Context

Ask:

Is the number still current?

Many political claims use outdated statistics.

Current data is preferred.

---

# Verify Headlines

News headlines alone are insufficient.

Read beyond:

* Social media posts
* Headlines
* Advocacy summaries

Verify the underlying facts.

---

# Legislative Verification

When discussing legislation:

Confirm:

* Bill number
* Vote date
* Vote outcome
* Candidate position
* Final disposition

Do not rely on summaries alone.

---

# Funding Claims

When discussing spending:

Specify:

* Amount
* Source
* Program
* Date

Bad:

"Spent millions."

Better:

"Supported a $12 million spending increase."

Precision increases credibility.

---

# Association Claims

Association is not endorsement.

Bad:

"Supported every position of Organization X."

Because:

A candidate attended an event.

Better:

"Accepted endorsement from Organization X."

Or:

"Appeared at an event hosted by Organization X."

State only what can be proven.

---

# Endorsement Claims

Verify:

* Endorsement exists
* Endorsement is current
* Endorsement was accepted

Do not assume support from proximity.

---

# Social Media Verification

Verify:

* Account ownership
* Date
* Context
* Authenticity

Screenshots alone are not enough.

Find the original source when possible.

---

# Fact Check Workflow

For every major claim:

Step 1

Identify the claim.

Step 2

Identify the evidence.

Step 3

Evaluate source quality.

Step 4

Determine confidence level.

Step 5

Rewrite if necessary.

---

# Confidence Ratings

## High Confidence

Supported by:

* Official records
* Votes
* Government documents
* Court filings

Safe for mail, digital, and earned media.

---

## Medium Confidence

Supported by:

* Credible reporting
* Public statements
* Verified secondary sources

Usually acceptable.

Consider additional sourcing.

---

## Low Confidence

Supported by:

* Social media
* Advocacy summaries
* Anonymous claims
* Indirect evidence

Use cautiously.

Seek stronger evidence.

---

# Red Flag Language

Review carefully when copy contains:

* Always
* Never
* Every
* Everyone
* Nobody
* Proven
* Corrupt
* Criminal
* Fraud
* Illegal
* Lied
* Extreme
* Radical

Absolute language requires stronger proof.

---

# Legal Risk Review

Before approval ask:

Can this claim be documented?

Can the source be produced?

Would the claim survive public scrutiny?

Would a reporter be able to verify it?

Would opposing counsel challenge it?

If yes, strengthen sourcing or rewrite.

---

# Safer Rewrites

Instead of:

"Foley lied."

Use:

"Foley's statements conflict with her record."

Instead of:

"Corrupt politician."

Use:

"Questions remain about her handling of taxpayer funds."

Instead of:

"Illegal activity."

Use:

"Investigators examined the activity."

State what is known.

Not what is assumed.

---

# Mailer QA Questions

For every piece ask:

* What is the strongest factual claim?
* What is the source?
* Can the source be produced immediately?
* Is the language accurate?
* Is the claim overstated?
* Is the statistic current?
* Is the quote exact?
* Would a reporter accept the evidence?
* Would an opponent have an easy rebuttal?
* Is there a safer version that remains persuasive?

---

# Final Rule

The most persuasive political claim is not the harshest claim.

It is the strongest claim that can be proven.

If a claim cannot be defended, it should not be used.
