# Caliber Headline Punch

Use these rules whenever creating, rewriting, testing, ranking, or improving headlines for:

- Political mail
- Digital ads
- Social graphics
- Fundraising
- Landing pages
- Earned media
- Candidate websites
- Door hangers
- Palm cards

The objective is simple:

**Make the voter stop.**

If the headline does not stop the voter, nothing underneath matters.

---

# Core Rule

A headline should communicate:

1. The argument
2. The emotion
3. The target

As quickly as possible.

A good headline works before the voter reads the body copy.

---

# The 3-Second Test

Ask:

Can a voter understand the message in 3 seconds?

If not:

Rewrite.

---

# The 7-Word Rule

Most strong political headlines are:

3–7 words.

Good:

- Wrong Choice For Riverside
- Radical Democrat Katrina Foley
- Taxpayers Deserve Better
- Families Pay The Price
- Failed Leadership. Higher Costs.
- Protect Women's Sports
- Putting Parents First

Weak:

- Long explanatory headlines
- Multi-sentence headlines
- Consultant-style headlines

---

# Punch Hierarchy

To strengthen a headline:

1. Remove words
2. Increase emotion
3. Increase specificity
4. Increase contrast
5. Increase consequences

---

# Rule 1: Cut The Setup

Bad:

"Katrina Foley Has Repeatedly Supported Policies That Hurt Families"

Better:

"Foley Hurt Families"

Better:

"Families Paid The Price"

The headline should not explain.

It should strike.

---

# Rule 2: Use Consequences

Voters care about outcomes.

Weak:

"Supported Higher Spending"

Better:

"Higher Spending. Higher Costs."

Weak:

"Opposed Public Safety Funding"

Better:

"Less Funding. Less Safety."

---

# Rule 3: Use Contrast

Strong headlines often create conflict.

Examples:

- Activists First. Families Last.
- Politics Over Public Safety.
- Special Interests Over Taxpayers.
- Bureaucrats Over Parents.
- Sacramento Over Riverside.

Contrast creates clarity.

---

# Rule 4: Use Emotional Compression

Instead of:

"Policies That Increased Housing Costs"

Use:

"Housing Costs Soared."

Instead of:

"Supported Tax Increases"

Use:

"Higher Taxes. Less Relief."

Emotion beats explanation.

---

# Rule 5: Prefer Verbs

Weak:

"Tax Increases"

Stronger:

"Raised Taxes"

Weak:

"Government Waste"

Stronger:

"Wasted Taxpayer Dollars"

Verbs create action.

---

# Rule 6: Name The Villain

For attacks:

Use the target whenever possible.

Weak:

"Higher Costs"

Better:

"Foley Raised Costs"

Weak:

"Failed Leadership"

Better:

"Foley Failed Riverside"

The attack should have a face.

---

# Rule 7: Use Sentence Fragments

Fragments often outperform full sentences.

Examples:

- Wrong Choice For Riverside.
- Failed Leadership.
- Higher Costs.
- Broken Promises.
- Radical Priorities.
- Families Last.

Fragments are faster.

---

# Rule 8: Escalate Stakes

Weak:

"Questionable Priorities"

Better:

"Families Paid The Price"

Weak:

"Bad Decisions"

Better:

"Riverside Paid For It"

Show consequences.

---

# Rule 9: Specific Beats Generic

Weak:

"Failed Policies"

Better:

"Raised Taxes"

Weak:

"Bad Priorities"

Better:

"Put Activists First"

Specific wins.

---

# Rule 10: Build Around Tension

Strong headlines often answer:

Who got hurt?

Who benefited?

What was lost?

What changed?

Examples:

- Taxpayers Lost.
- Politicians Won.
- Parents Were Ignored.
- Activists Got Their Way.
- Families Paid More.

---

# Headline Structures

## Structure A

Target + Action

Examples:

- Foley Raised Costs.
- Foley Backed Higher Taxes.
- Foley Failed Families.

---

## Structure B

Consequence

Examples:

- Families Paid The Price.
- Costs Kept Rising.
- Riverside Deserves Better.

---

## Structure C

Contrast

Examples:

- Activists First. Families Last.
- Politics Over Public Safety.
- Bureaucrats Over Parents.

---

## Structure D

Warning

Examples:

- Wrong Choice For Riverside.
- Don't Trust The Record.
- We Can't Afford More.

---

## Structure E

Positive

Examples:

- A Proven Conservative Fighter.
- Protecting What Matters.
- Results You Can Trust.
- Leading With Common Sense.

---

# Negative Headline Escalation

Level 1

Questionable Priorities

Level 2

Failed Priorities

Level 3

Families Paid The Price

Level 4

Wrong Choice For Riverside

Move up only when evidence supports it.

---

# Positive Headline Escalation

Level 1

Conservative Leader

Level 2

Trusted Conservative Leader

Level 3

A Proven Conservative Fighter

Level 4

The Conservative Riverside Can Count On

Build confidence, not hype.

---

# A/B Testing Rules

Generate:

- One direct headline
- One emotional headline
- One contrast headline
- One consequence headline
- One aggressive headline

Never generate five versions that say the same thing.

---

# Headline Scoring System

Score 1–5 in each category.

## Clarity

Can voters understand it immediately?

## Emotion

Does it create a feeling?

## Specificity

Is it concrete?

## Contrast

Is there tension?

## Memorability

Will voters remember it?

Highest total wins.

---

# Red Flags

Avoid:

- Consultant language
- Policy jargon
- Long explanations
- Generic praise
- Generic attacks
- Passive voice
- Weak verbs

If the headline sounds like a committee wrote it, rewrite it.

---

# Final Rule

The best headline is usually not the smartest headline.

It is the headline voters remember after they throw the mailer away.
