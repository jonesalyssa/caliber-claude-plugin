# Caliber Client Communication Style

This skill captures how Caliber principals communicate creative direction to designers, based on real client-designer exchanges. Use it to interpret client briefs, anticipate feedback patterns, and align design output with how this client thinks.

---

## How This Client Gives Direction

**They think in layers, not specs.** Initial briefs are conceptual and visual — they describe a feeling or a scene ("make it look like the yard sign is flowing out of the drain pipe") rather than precise measurements. Fill in the production gaps yourself; don't wait to be asked.

**They iterate fast.** Expect multiple rounds of quick feedback. They send a reaction ("Yes!!!!" / "Love it" / "This is bad ass"), then pivot immediately to the next problem. Don't over-explain revisions — just make them and show the result.

**They flag copy details precisely.** When copy is wrong, they catch it exactly: wrong capitalization, missing period, wrong word. These are not suggestions — fix them immediately. Examples from this client: "make 'AGAINST' all caps," "we gotta add a period," "put her last name in the news clip section."

**They use reference visuals to communicate intent.** When stuck on layout, they send an existing mailer or comp as a reference. Read the structure, not just the content — they want to apply the *architecture* of the reference to the current piece.

**They respond to designer questions with judgment calls.** When a designer asks "is this distracting or does it help?" they engage directly: "This is bad ass. Let's roll." They trust their gut and move.

## Creative Preferences Observed

### Contrast mailers
- Candidate's name in large, bold type — dominant, not decorative
- Attack copy goes on yellow highlight bars with black text
- Key attack word (e.g., "AGAINST," "ONLY") gets emphasis treatment: all caps, italic, or underline
- News clips formatted as mock TV chyrons or newspaper cutouts — source logo, date, quote in box
- "Voted NO" or vote direction CTA goes at the bottom in a yellow bar, bold
- Taglines in italic: *She's All Talk. No Action.*
- Disclaimer block at very bottom, small, centered

### Photo/image direction
- Candidate photo: desaturated or duotone to match grim mood of attack
- Background image should reinforce the attack visually (sewage drain, polluted beach, dirty yard sign)
- Composites should look editorial, not clipped — photo needs to feel like it belongs in the scene
- Yard signs used as props: weathered, tilted, partially submerged, or carried away — never clean and upright
- Subject's face and body should face into the layout, not away from text

### Layout patterns
- Mailing side: dominant image + candidate name + one attack line — clean and fast to read
- Non-mailing side: layered — news clip at top right, headline block center, photo left, CTA bar bottom
- Text on right, image on left (default for non-mailing side)
- Mailing panel: top right placement for indicia/label

### Typography
- Candidate name: heavy condensed serif or impact-style sans — all caps, large
- Attack line: mixed case bold inside yellow bar, key word emphasized
- News clip text: smaller, styled to match source (KUSI NEWS aesthetic)
- Body support copy: clean sans-serif, sentence case
- Two lines at same size when they function as a unit — don't let one dominate if they're equal weight

## Designer Workflow Patterns

**This client checks in frequently.** They want to see work-in-progress, not just finals. Send intermediate comps with a "How is this?" and expect a quick yes/no with specific notes.

**They give designers creative latitude within constraints.** "I'll defer to you on placement" means they've set the constraint (sewage pipe on the left, label top right) and trust execution on the rest.

**When a designer is stuck, they redirect with reference.** "Take a look at those mailers — I think you'll find something you like there." Keep a reference library of approved Caliber work to pull from.

**They catch missing elements on proofread.** Always confirm: candidate last name present, period at end of CTA, "only" underlined if used in headline, disclaimer complete.

## Quick Reference: This Client's Language

| What they say | What they mean |
|---|---|
| "I'll defer to you" | You have creative freedom within the constraint I just gave you |
| "Let's make it look like X" | Pursue this visual metaphor literally |
| "I think we're close" | One or two specific changes, then done |
| "Let's roll!" | Approved — move to final |
| "Give me a moment" | They're writing detailed revision notes |
| "I kind of like the way they do X" | Use this as a direct reference for this element |
| "This is bad ass" | Strong approval, no changes needed on this element |
