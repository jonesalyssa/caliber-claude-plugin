# Contrast Rules

Use these rules for any Caliber mailer, palm card, door hanger, digital graphic, or concept that needs stronger visual contrast, readability, or voter-facing hierarchy.

## Core Rule

Contrast is not just color. Contrast means the voter instantly knows:

1. Who the piece is about
2. What the main message is
3. What is good vs. bad
4. What to read first
5. What matters most

If the voter has to hunt, the contrast failed.

## Readability Comes First

Senior-voter readability is non-negotiable.

- Body copy must be at least 12pt.
- Use dark text on light backgrounds by default.
- Avoid reverse type for body copy, bullets, small subheads, captions, and pull quotes.
- Reverse type is only acceptable for large, bold, high-contrast headlines.
- Copy should never fight a busy texture, photo, gradient, or background.
- Use white boxes, torn-paper strips, soft gradients, or clean panels behind copy when needed.
- If a 65-year-old cannot read it at arm’s length, fix it.

## Light vs. Dark Backgrounds

Default to light backgrounds with dark text.

Use dark backgrounds only when the mood earns it, usually for:

- Attack pieces
- Contrast pieces
- Corruption themes
- Crime/public safety themes
- “Wrong choice” or warning-style creative

Do not use dark backgrounds just because the piece feels empty. Positive pieces should usually feel bright, clean, patriotic, and easy to read.

## Visual Contrast

For contrast or attack pieces, the visual should help make the argument before the voter reads the headline.

Strong contrast treatments include:

- Our candidate bright, warm, sharp, and clear
- Opponent muted, desaturated, washed out, shadowed, or boxed in
- Good side clean and open
- Bad side crowded, dark, chaotic, or visually contaminated
- Positive record in clean panels
- Negative record in warning labels, case files, torn paper, surveillance frames, receipts, or evidence-style treatments

The image should not be decorative. It should carry the message.

## Hierarchy Contrast

The most important message must look most important.

Check:

- Is the headline dominant?
- Is the candidate name unmissable?
- Is the opponent clearly the target?
- Are bullets secondary to the headline?
- Is the disclaimer visually quiet?
- Are sources readable but not competing?
- Are too many elements fighting for first place?

If everything is bold, nothing is bold.

## Copy Contrast

Use contrast in the wording too.

Good contrast copy is:

- Short
- Specific
- Voter-facing
- Easy to scan
- Built around clear opposites

Examples of useful contrast structures:

- Local vs. Outside
- Words vs. Actions
- Then vs. Now
- Voters vs. Insiders
- Record vs. Record
- Safe vs. Dangerous
- Taxpayers vs. Politicians
- Families vs. Woke Agenda
- America First vs. Liberal Agenda

Avoid vague phrases like:

- “Common-sense solutions”
- “Standing up for families”
- “Fighting for values”
- “Delivering results”
- “Putting people first”

Unless the claim is made concrete immediately.

## Bullet Contrast

Bullets should be visually and verbally scannable.

Rules:

- Keep bullets short.
- Use parallel structure.
- Start with strong verbs or direct nouns.
- Avoid full sentences when a phrase will do.
- Do not let bullets become paragraphs.
- Use icons only if they help scanning.
- Do not use low-contrast icon colors.
- Make sure each bullet has equal visual weight.

Bad:

- “Has consistently supported policies that would make it harder for families to afford everyday expenses.”

Better:

- “Backed higher costs for working families.”

## Positive vs. Negative Contrast

On two-sided pieces, the positive side and negative side should connect.

Do not let the front talk about one issue while the back attacks on a different issue unless the strategy requires it.

Better structure:

- Back: Opponent made life more expensive.
- Front: Our candidate will cut taxes and lower costs.

Weaker structure:

- Back: Opponent supports woke programs.
- Front: Our candidate supports veterans and small business.

The sides should hand off to each other.

## Color Contrast

Use color to organize meaning.

Common Caliber logic:

- Red = warning, urgency, wrong choice, attack
- Navy = authority, seriousness, patriotism
- White = clarity, cleanliness, trust
- Gold/tan = heritage, warmth, rural/conservative polish
- Black/gray = corruption, investigation, threat, heaviness

Do not put dark text on dark texture or white text on pale texture. Contrast must survive print.

## Texture Contrast

Texture should add emotion without killing legibility.

Use texture for:

- Grit
- Age
- Corruption
- Evidence
- Western/rural character
- Newspaper/editorial feel
- Surveillance/case-file mood

But keep text areas clean.

If texture is behind copy, add:

- White panel
- Light gradient
- Torn paper strip
- Blur/softened overlay
- Lower-opacity background
- Solid block behind text

## Photo Contrast

Photo treatment should support the message.

Positive candidate photos:

- Warm
- Bright
- Clear
- Human
- Confident
- Hero-style
- Cleanly cut out

Opponent/attack photos:

- Desaturated
- Muted
- Grainy
- Lower contrast in face but high contrast around frame
- Boxed, stamped, circled, taped, filed, or surveilled
- Visually placed in the “bad” side of the composition

Do not over-edit faces in a way that looks fake. The treatment should feel political, not cartoonish unless the concept is intentionally satirical.

## Spacing Contrast

Contrast also comes from breathing room.

- Leave space around the main headline.
- Do not crowd the candidate’s face.
- Keep gutters consistent.
- Match widths of parallel panels.
- Align bullets cleanly.
- Keep source/disclaimer areas controlled.
- Do not let design elements touch the trim or bleed danger zone.

Sloppy spacing makes the piece feel cheap.

## “More Contrast” Usually Means

When someone says “more contrast,” diagnose which kind they mean:

1. Color contrast — text/background is too similar
2. Hierarchy contrast — headline does not dominate
3. Message contrast — good vs. bad is not clear enough
4. Visual contrast — image does not match the attack
5. Emotional contrast — piece feels too neutral
6. Format contrast — too many elements have the same size/weight
7. Readability contrast — copy is getting lost

Do not just darken everything. Fix the actual contrast problem.

## Fast Fixes

Use these when a design feels flat:

- Make the headline bigger and simpler.
- Put body copy on a white or light panel.
- Desaturate the opponent photo.
- Add a warning/stamp/tape/evidence treatment to the negative side.
- Increase spacing between sections.
- Reduce the number of fonts and weights.
- Make the good side cleaner and the bad side messier.
- Add a clear red/navy/white hierarchy.
- Remove decorative elements that compete with the message.
- Make the CTA or core contrast impossible to miss.

## QA Checklist

Before approval, check:

- Can the main message be understood in 3 seconds?
- Is the headline readable from arm’s length?
- Is body copy at least 12pt?
- Is any small copy in reverse type? If yes, fix it.
- Does the background interfere with reading?
- Does the visual support the message?
- Is the candidate/opponent hierarchy clear?
- Are good vs. bad visually distinct?
- Are panels, bullets, and source areas aligned?
- Does the piece still work in print, not just on screen?

## Default Recommendation

When in doubt:

Use a light, clean copy area with dark text, one dominant headline, one clear visual argument, and enough spacing for the voter to understand the piece instantly.
