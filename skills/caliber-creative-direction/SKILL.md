# Caliber Creative Direction

Use these rules whenever generating concepts, art direction, design direction, layout recommendations, mail themes, digital ad concepts, fundraising creative, or campaign visual strategies.

The goal is not to create art.

The goal is to create persuasion.

Creative exists to communicate political arguments faster and more memorably than text alone.

---

# Core Philosophy

Every creative decision should answer:

**What argument is this helping make?**

If the answer is unclear, the creative is decorative.

Decorative creative loses elections.

Persuasive creative wins them.

---

# The Three-Second Rule

A voter should understand:

1. Who the piece is about
2. Whether the piece is positive or negative
3. The main argument

Within three seconds.

If the voter needs to read body copy to understand the piece, the creative concept is too weak.

---

# Concept Before Design

Never start with:

- Colors
- Fonts
- Layout
- Effects

Start with:

- The argument
- The contrast
- The emotion

Then build the creative around it.

---

# The Big Question

Before generating concepts ask:

"What should the voter feel?"

Common answers:

- Trust
- Pride
- Anger
- Concern
- Hope
- Frustration
- Confidence
- Urgency
- Relief

Emotion determines creative direction.

---

# Positive Creative

Positive pieces should feel:

- Confident
- Optimistic
- Forward-looking
- Competent
- Human

Common themes:

- Community
- Family
- Small business
- Public service
- Accomplishment
- Leadership
- Opportunity
- Local pride

Positive creative should create aspiration.

---

# Negative Creative

Negative creative should feel:

- Serious
- Memorable
- Credible
- Uncomfortable

Not:

- Cartoonish
- Silly
- Overly dramatic

The voter should believe the criticism.

Not laugh at it.

---

# Concept Selection Framework

Strong concepts usually fit one of these categories.

---

# Investigation

Use for:

- Corruption
- Ethics
- Scandals
- Financial issues

Examples:

- Evidence boards
- Case files
- Surveillance
- Investigative reports
- Redactions
- Financial records
- Receipts

Message:

"Something is wrong here."

---

# Warning

Use for:

- Risk
- Public safety
- Radical policies
- Failed leadership

Examples:

- Stamps
- Warning labels
- Hazard graphics
- Alert systems
- Red flags

Message:

"Pay attention before it's too late."

---

# Financial

Use for:

- Taxes
- Spending
- Inflation
- Waste

Examples:

- Burning money
- Receipts
- Bills
- Taxpayer wallets
- Government spending

Message:

"Your money is at risk."

---

# Comparison

Use for:

- Candidate contrasts
- Head-to-head pieces

Examples:

- Split layouts
- Before and after
- Side-by-side records
- Good versus bad

Message:

"These choices are not the same."

---

# Public Safety

Use for:

- Crime
- Border security
- Law enforcement

Examples:

- Evidence
- Police imagery
- Security themes
- Community protection

Message:

"Safety is at stake."

---

# Local Pride

Use for:

- Incumbents
- Popular elected officials
- Local leaders

Examples:

- Landmarks
- Main streets
- Small businesses
- Community events

Message:

"This candidate belongs here."

---

# Rural & Western

Use for:

- Agricultural communities
- Western states
- Resource issues

Examples:

- Ranching
- Public lands
- Open landscapes
- Working families
- Natural resources

Message:

"Understands our way of life."

---

# Visual Metaphors

The best concepts use visual metaphors.

Examples:

Corruption → Evidence board

Waste → Money burning

Failure → Cracks, collapse, decline

Leadership → Forward movement

Transparency → Open records

Danger → Warning systems

The metaphor should reinforce the argument.

Never choose random aesthetics.

---

# Candidate Treatment

## Positive Candidate

Use:

- Bright lighting
- Strong posture
- Clean cutouts
- Clear focus
- Natural expressions

The candidate should look capable and approachable.

---

## Negative Target

Use:

- Desaturated treatment
- Investigative framing
- Surveillance overlays
- Warning graphics
- Case-file presentation

Do not distort faces.

Do not create fake imagery.

Credibility matters.

---

# Layout Strategy

Always establish:

Primary focus

Secondary support

Tertiary details

The eye should know where to go.

Common order:

Headline

Candidate/target

Visual concept

Bullets

Supporting copy

Sources

Disclaimer

---

# Originality Rules

Do not repeat concepts simply because they worked before.

Ask:

What is a different way to tell the same story?

If this is:

Mailer #1

Simple concepts work.

If this is:

Mailer #10

The concept must feel distinct.

The argument can remain the same.

The presentation should evolve.

---

# "Completely Different" Framework

When asked for a completely different concept:

Do not change:

- The message
- The argument
- The target

Change:

- Visual metaphor
- Layout structure
- Environment
- Presentation style
- Mood
- Framing device

Example:

Corruption can become:

- Evidence board
- Surveillance room
- Newspaper investigation
- Financial collapse
- Government waste
- Ethics complaint
- Audit file
- Receipt trail

Same argument.

Different execution.

---

# Avoid These Mistakes

Do not:

- Decorate without purpose
- Add clutter
- Use trendy effects
- Create busy backgrounds
- Overcomplicate layouts
- Force symbolism
- Make concepts harder to understand

The concept should simplify the message.

Not complicate it.

---

# Creative Review Questions

Before approval ask:

What is the argument?

What is the emotion?

What is the visual metaphor?

Could a voter understand this in three seconds?

Does the concept reinforce the copy?

Is anything decorative instead of persuasive?

Can anything be removed?

Would this stand out in a stack of mail?

---

# Default Recommendation

When in doubt:

Use a simple, emotionally clear visual metaphor tied directly to the argument.

The strongest creative concepts are usually the simplest ones.
